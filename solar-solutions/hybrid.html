The Basics

What Is a Hybrid Solar System?

A grid-connected set-up with battery storage that combines the savings of on-grid solar with the reliable backup of off-grid — the best of both technologies in one system.

A hybrid system runs in sync with the utility grid like an on-grid system, but adds a battery so surplus solar can be stored instead of only exported. During the day your panels power the home, top up the battery and send any extra to the grid for credits. In the evening — or during a power cut — the battery takes over, so you are never left in the dark. Every hybrid system is built from these core building blocks:

  • Solar panels (modules) — capture sunlight and convert it to DC electricity
  • Hybrid solar inverter — manages panels, battery and grid all at once
  • Battery bank — stores surplus solar for the evening and outages
  • Mounting structure — holds the panels at the right tilt and orientation
  • Bi-directional (net) meter — measures both imported and exported units
  • Balance of system — cabling, protection, earthing and accessories
Under the Hood

The Components of a Hybrid Solar System

Let’s break the system down component by component so you understand the role each one plays.

1. Solar Panels

Made of solar cells — monocrystalline, polycrystalline or bifacial (cells on front and rear). They convert sunlight into DC power; the key during installation is making sure no shadows fall on them.

2. Hybrid Solar Inverter

The brain of the system. A single smart inverter intelligently routes power between your home, the battery and the grid — charging storage when there is surplus and switching to backup the instant the grid drops.

3. Battery Bank

Stores surplus solar for use after sunset and during outages. Modern lithium (LFP) batteries offer long life, deep discharge and compact size, while lead-acid remains a lower-cost option.

4. Bi-directional Meter

A net meter records electricity flowing both ways — the units you export to the grid and the units you import. Your bill is calculated on the net of the two, the heart of net metering.

5. Mounting Structure

Holds everything in place. A good structure keeps panels at the right tilt — near-perpendicular to the sun for as long as possible — and is engineered to survive local wind and weather conditions.

6. Cables, BoS & Accessories

Specialised DC & AC cables, combiner boxes, MC4 connectors plus the safety essentials — earthing, surge protection and a lightning arrester — that carry power safely and protect your home.

How It Works

How Does a Hybrid Solar System Work?

From a photon hitting a panel to a battery that carries you through the night — here is the full journey.

1

Solar Energy Capture & DC Generation

Photovoltaic cells in the panels absorb sunlight and convert it directly into direct current (DC) electricity throughout the day.

2

Household Consumption First

The hybrid inverter converts the solar DC to clean AC and powers your appliances instantly — your home always uses its own free solar power first.

3

Charging the Battery

Once your home is powered, any surplus is used to charge the battery bank — storing free daytime solar for use in the evening.

4

Exporting the Remaining Surplus

When the battery is full and the home is powered, the leftover units are exported to the grid through the net meter for export credits.

5

Battery & Grid After Sunset

In the evening your home runs on the stored battery power first, drawing from the grid only if the battery is depleted — offset by your export credits.

6

Instant Backup During Outages

If the grid fails, the hybrid inverter switches to battery power in milliseconds, so your essential loads keep running without interruption.

The Trade-offs

Advantages & Disadvantages of Hybrid Solar

Hybrid is the right choice when you want savings and backup — but it is worth weighing both sides before you commit.

Advantages

  • Power during outages. The battery keeps your home running 24/7, even when the grid is down.
  • Government subsidy. The solar capacity is eligible for up to ₹78,000 central subsidy under PM Surya Ghar.
  • Maximise self-use. Store free daytime solar for the evening, then export the rest for net-metering credits.
  • Energy independence. Less reliance on the grid and complete protection from rising tariffs and outages.

Disadvantages

  • Higher upfront cost. The battery and smart inverter make hybrid pricier than a plain on-grid system.
  • Battery replacement. Batteries last 8–15 years and will eventually need replacing, an added lifetime cost.
  • Slightly longer payback. The extra hardware means a modestly longer payback period than on-grid.
