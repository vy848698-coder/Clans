An On-Grid (or Grid-Tied) Solar System is the most popular, cost-effective, and widely used solar setup for residential homes and commercial buildings.

No batteries needed

The grid is your battery

Instead of storing power in costly batteries, you bank surplus units on the grid and draw them back later. That makes on-grid the cheapest and fastest-payback way to go solar.

Earn while you save

Net metering cuts up to 90%

A bi-directional meter tracks every unit you export and import. You are billed only on the net difference — which is how on-grid systems routinely wipe out 80–90% of a monthly bill.

Step-by-Step

How It Works

1

Solar Energy Generation

High-efficiency rooftop solar panels absorb daylight photons and instantly convert them into Direct Current (DC) electricity.

2

Power Inversion

The standard DC electricity travels into a smart On-Grid Inverter, which smoothly converts it into Alternating Current (AC) electricity to safely power your appliances.

3

Immediate Home Consumption

Your home automatically utilizes this solar-generated AC power first. If your house needs more power than the panels are generating, the system silently pulls the balance from the regular grid.

4

Net-Metering & Credit Banking

When your solar panels generate more power than your home is using, the excess electricity flows back through a special Bidirectional Net-Meter to the utility grid. Your DISCOM tracks this surplus and deducts it from your monthly power statement.

The Trade-offs

Advantages & Disadvantages of On-Grid Solar

On-grid is the right choice for most homes with a reliable grid — but it is worth weighing both sides before you commit.

Advantages

  • Lowest upfront cost. No battery means the cheapest way to go solar, with the fastest payback.
  • Government subsidy. Eligible for up to ₹78,000 central subsidy under PM Surya Ghar.
  • Earn from surplus. Net metering credits every extra unit you export to the grid.
  • Low maintenance. With no batteries to replace, upkeep is minimal over 25 years.

Disadvantages

  • No backup during outages. For safety, the system shuts off when the grid goes down (unless you add a hybrid battery).
  • Grid dependence. You still need a working grid connection and an approved net-metering arrangement.
  • Discom approval needed. Net metering requires sanction from your local electricity board, which we handle for you.

The main difference comes down to batteries, the grid connection and how each handles a power cut.

If your area has a dependable grid, on-grid is almost always the smarter buy. If your grid is absent or very unreliable, an off-grid solar system with battery backup is the better fit.
