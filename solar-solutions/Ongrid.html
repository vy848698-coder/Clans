The Basics

What Is an On-Grid Solar System?

A grid-connected set-up that generates electricity from sunlight and runs in sync with the utility grid — using the grid as a virtual battery for the surplus it produces.

When your panels produce more than your home is using, the extra power flows back into the grid and you earn credits for it. When they produce less — at night or on cloudy days — you simply draw what you need from the grid. There are no batteries to buy, maintain or replace, which keeps the system affordable. Every grid-tied system is built from the same core building blocks:

  • Solar panels (modules) — capture sunlight and convert it to DC electricity
  • Grid-tie solar inverter — converts DC to grid-synchronised AC
  • Mounting structure — holds the panels at the right tilt and orientation
  • Bi-directional (net) meter — measures both imported and exported units
  • Balance of system — AC/DC cables, combiner boxes and protection
  • Solar accessories — earthing, conduits and a lightning arrester
Under the Hood

The Components of an On-Grid Solar System

Let’s break the system down component by component so you understand the role each one plays.

1. Solar Panels

Made of solar cells — monocrystalline, polycrystalline or bifacial (cells on front and rear). They convert sunlight into DC power; the key during installation is making sure no shadows fall on them.

2. Grid-Tie Solar Inverter

The brain of the system. It converts panel DC into clean AC that is perfectly synchronised with the grid’s voltage and frequency, and shuts down safely during a grid outage (anti-islanding) to protect line workers.

3. Bi-directional Meter

A net meter records electricity flowing both ways — the units you export to the grid and the units you import. Your bill is calculated on the net of the two, the heart of net metering.

4. Mounting Structure

Holds everything in place. A good structure keeps panels at the right tilt — near-perpendicular to the sun for as long as possible — and is engineered to survive local wind and weather conditions.

5. Solar Cables & Balance of System

Specialised DC & AC cables, combiner boxes and MC4 connectors carry power safely and with minimal loss from the panels through the inverter and out to your distribution board.

6. Solar Accessories

The safety essentials that protect the system and your home: earthing strips, conduit trays, surge protection devices and a lightning arrester.

How It Works

How Does an On-Grid Solar System Work?

From a photon hitting a panel to credits on your electricity bill — here is the full journey.

1

Solar Energy Capture & DC Generation

Photovoltaic cells in the panels absorb sunlight and convert it directly into direct current (DC) electricity throughout the day.

2

DC-to-AC Conversion via the Inverter

The grid-tie inverter converts that DC into clean 230V AC, perfectly synchronised with the grid’s voltage and frequency.

3

Household Consumption First

The solar AC powers your appliances, lights and devices instantly — your home always uses its own free solar power before anything else.

4

Exporting the Surplus to the Grid

When you generate more than you use, the bi-directional meter sends the extra units to the grid and records them as export credits.

5

Importing Grid Power When Needed

At night or under heavy load, your home seamlessly draws from the grid — offset by the credits you banked earlier in the day.

6

Net Billing at Month End

The discom bills you only on the net difference between units imported and exported — which is how your bill drops to near zero.

The Trade-offs

Advantages & Disadvantages of On-Grid Solar

On-grid is the right choice for most homes with a reliable grid — but it is worth weighing both sides before you commit.

Advantages

  • Lowest upfront cost. No battery means the cheapest way to go solar, with the fastest payback.
  • Government subsidy. Eligible for up to ₹78,000 central subsidy under PM Surya Ghar.
  • Earn from surplus. Net metering credits every extra unit you export to the grid.
  • Low maintenance. With no batteries to replace, upkeep is minimal over 25 years.

Disadvantages

  • No backup during outages. For safety, the system shuts off when the grid goes down (unless you add a hybrid battery).
  • Grid dependence. You still need a working grid connection and an approved net-metering arrangement.
  • Discom approval needed. Net metering requires sanction from your local electricity board, which we handle for you.

If your area has a dependable grid, on-grid is almost always the smarter buy. If your grid is absent or very unreliable, an off-grid solar system with battery backup is the better fit.
